Key trends

What's changing — and why it matters

Interest on the debt crossed $1 trillion for the first time in FY 2025 — now larger than either Medicare or defense spending alone. It is the fastest-growing line item in the federal budget, outpacing both Social Security and Medicare.

Source: CBO Monthly Budget Review, Nov 2025

Publicly held debt reached 99.8% of GDP in 2025, up from 97.4% in 2024. The GAO projects it will exceed its post-WWII record of 106% of GDP by 2027 — one year earlier than previously forecast — and reach 200% of GDP by 2047.

Source: GAO Fiscal Health Report, Feb 2025

Mandatory spending and interest combined are projected to equal roughly all federal revenue by FY 2026, meaning every dollar of discretionary spending — including defense and education — would effectively be deficit-financed.

Source: Bipartisan Policy Center, Apr 2026

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (2025) is estimated by the CBO to add $4.1 trillion to the federal debt through 2034 ($3.4T in primary deficits plus $718B in interest costs), partially offset by $2.5T in cuts and revenue increases.

Source: CBO, via CRFB, 2025

Over the next decade, more than 80% of spending growth is projected to come from three sources: Social Security, federal health programs (primarily Medicare), and net interest. All other spending combined accounts for less than 20% of projected growth.

Source: Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget, Jan 2025

Where we get our data

Nonpartisan sources only

All statistics in this group must trace back to official government data or established nonpartisan institutions. Here are the primary ones we use.

CBO ↗
Congressional Budget Office — the official nonpartisan scorekeeper for federal budget and economic projections.
Treasury ↗
US Treasury Fiscal Data — official government spending, revenue, and debt figures.
GAO ↗
Government Accountability Office — independent federal audits and long-range fiscal projections.
Peterson Foundation ↗
Nonpartisan fiscal education and data visualization. Good for accessible explainers.
CRFB ↗
Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget — nonpartisan analysis of fiscal legislation.
Bipartisan Policy Center ↗
Cross-party research and data on federal budget structure and fiscal trends.

All figures reflect FY 2025 actuals where available, otherwise the most recent official estimates. This page does not advocate for any political position. Sources: CBO, GAO, US Treasury, Peterson Foundation, CRFB, Bipartisan Policy Center. Last updated June 2026.
